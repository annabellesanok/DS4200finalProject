Visualizing Fundamental and Stock Data of S&P 500 Companies

Project Introduction

In this project, we delve into a comparison of business fundamentals across leading companies in the technology and healthcare sectors of the S&P 500. The core of our investigation focuses on fundamental analysis—evaluating the financial health, competitive positioning, and potential for future shareholder returns to gauge a company's intrinsic value. Fundamental ratios and indicators are important in assessing whether a stock is trading above or below its true value. We aim to bring these financial metrics to life through our visualizations to assess what drives stock prices and how financials of healthcare and tech industries inform prevailing business models.  While these visualizations won’t lead to concrete investment decisions, they can be used as a starting point to base investing frameworks off of. Our visualizations will navigate through the volatility and steadiness of market trends to provide a deeper understanding of the disparities in business fundamentals between tech and healthcare entities and the various factors influencing stock prices over time. Through our findings, we hope to offer insights into the relationship between a company's fundamental soundness and its perceived value in the marketplace.

Overall Data Visualization

Our project explores fundamental financial data for selected healthcare and technology stocks within the S&P 500, spanning from 2009 to 2023. Our dataset offers an extensive range of metrics, including closing price, volume, net earnings per share (EPS), sales per share, and price-to-earnings (PE) ratio, across 33 different financial indicators. Each stock's data points, recorded approximately five times per month, result in approximately 2,500 rows per stock.

Our data includes prominent companies in the healthcare sector such as AbbVie (ABBV), Abbott Laboratories (ABT), AstraZeneca (AZN), Bristol-Myers (BMY), Danaher (DHR), Johnson & Johnson (JNJ), Eli Lilly (LLY), Merck (MRK), and Pfizer (PFE). The technology segment is represented by tech companies like Apple (AAPL), Broadcom (AVGO), Cisco Systems (CSCO), Alphabet (GOOGL), Microsoft (MSFT), Nvidia (NVDA), and Oracle (ORCL).

Initially, we ingested data from multiple CSV files into individual dataframes, while also appending a new column labeled 'Industry' to categorize each entry into Healthcare and Tech. Then we grouped each financial metric by quarter to average the values. Finally, we merged these dataframes into a single comprehensive dataframe, which we used to create our visualizations.

The visualization above showcases the trajectory of closing prices over time for each ticker within each industry. It reveals the growth patterns and trends of individual companies, and also provides a comparative insight into the industry’s dynamics over the years. Through our line chart, it becomes evident that while some stocks have shown consistent upward momentum, others display more volatile trajectories. The distinct color coding for each ticker allows for clearer comparison across the different companies, and the interactive dropdown allows the user to toggle between industries. The closing prices of healthcare companies, as shown in the visualization, depict the impact of market conditions, company performance, and sector-specific events on stock valuations over the period.

Static Data Visualizations

Boxplot

This boxplot provides a comparative analysis of debt-to-equity ratios across each of the industries. This metric is useful in understanding how companies leverage debt relative to their equity, which is a key indicator of their financial health and risk management strategy.

In the healthcare industry, companies typically operate with consistent and predictable cash flows due to the constant demand for healthcare services and pharmaceutical products. This sector's boxplot showcases a more compact IQR, indicative of less variance in how these companies manage their debt levels. The stability of cash flows in this sector allows for healthcare companies to sustain higher debt loads. This provides them with a strategic advantage as it can be used to fund ongoing research, expansion, and operations without jeopardizing financial stability.

The tech industry exhibits a broader range of debt-to-equity ratios, as reflected by a wider spread of data points in the boxplot. This diversity is indicative of the varied business models and stages of growth within the tech sector. While a number of tech companies maintain moderate leverage, demonstrating a cautious approach to debt, there are outliers that deviate significantly from the norm. These outliers could represent aggressive growth strategies through debt financing or could signal companies encountering financial challenges.

The whiskers of the boxplot extend to capture the full range of values, but there are several outliers to note. In the case of tech companies, these outliers could be younger, high-growth companies investing heavily in innovation, or established firms diversifying through acquisitions — both scenarios where debt might temporarily outpace equity.

Current Asset & Liability Across Industries

This visualization shows three different key values: current asset, current libaility, and working capital across the two selected industries, tech and healthcare. Between the three values, current assets represent the resources a company can use to fund its operations, current liabilities represent a company's short-term financial obligations that needs to be paid off from the current assets, and working capital, which is current assets minus current liabilities, represents how much liquid assets a company has after paying off its short-term debts.

Between the three recorded values, we're most interested in comparing working capital between the two industries. Working capital is an important measure of a company's liquidity, operational efficiency, and short-term financial health. Companies with high working capital can better manage their cash flows, take advantage of opportunities, and withstand financial hardships in the short run. A positive working capital means the company has enough current assets to cover its current liabilities and continue operations. A negative working capital indicates the company may struggle to pay off its short-term obligations with its existing current assets.

When comparing the working capital of tech and healthcare companies, we noticed that healthcare companies tend to have a lower working capital than those of tech companies. We suspect that this is so because healthcare, as an industry, is much more mature than tech, and therefore are able to take on more short-term debts (hence the higher current liabilities as well). Another interesting aspect of our data is that although tech has higher overall working capital, the downward trending line of best fit shows that the industry is slowly maturing/stablizing. Perhaps the delayed growth of the industry's current assets will have an latened affect on the current liabilities.

PE vs. PFCF Ratios

This bubble chart was created using D3. Rather than comparing each of the industries as a whole, we decided to look into a few of the ratios within just tech companies to understand how they compare to one another.

The horizontal axis represents the PE (price to earnings) ratio, which measures a company's current share price relative to its per-share earnings. A higher PE ratio may suggest that a company's stock is overvalued, or investors are expecting high growth rates in the future. The vertical axis shows the PFCF (price to free cash flow) ratio, indicating how much investors are willing to pay per dollar of free cash flow. Lower values typically signify better value. Each bubble's size reflects the company's ROE (return on equity), which measures how much profit a company generates with the money shareholders have invested. Larger bubbles denote higher ROE, suggesting more efficient use of equity.

NVDA (Nvidia) demonstrates a high PE ratio and a moderate PFCF ratio with a high ROE value. Nvidia’s high PE can be attributed to the market’s high expectations for growth, especially given its dominant position in the GPU market and its prospects in AI and deep learning industries. Its high ROE is indicative of effective management, which can justify the higher PE ratio.

A company like CSCO (Cisco Systems) shows moderate PE and lower PFCF ratios with moderate ROE bubble size. It is a mature technology company with a steady cash flow, so its valuation may not be as high as more growth-oriented tech firms.

Interactive Data Visualizations

PE vs. EPS

This visualization reconciles investors’ expectations (price-to-earnings) with reality (earnings per share). In this case, the price-to-earnings ratio is supposed to gauge what investors think a stock/company will earn in the future, while the earnings-per-share is how much a company earned over the last year.

Increases in a stock’s PE ratio means that investors expect this company to do better in the future, while decreases in PE ratio reflects souring sentiment over a company’s prospects. A lot of the time, it can be observed that as a company’s EPS increased, PE increased as well, which shows that as a company earns more, investors update their beliefs about a company to reflect that its profitability is growing and will likely continue to grow in the future.

However, the observed relationship is not always the case. There are many instances throughout the graphs where sharp increases or decreases in EPS seemed to have the opposite effect on PE. This is because investors are forward looking. Investors are able to parse when sharp increases or decreases in earnings are essentially one-time events. A one-time increase in EPS, for example, does not necessarily mean that this is the level of earnings that should be expected of the company in the future. EPS is not a perfect metric of pure, “steady-state” operating profitability, as it can be impacted by one-time tax breaks or interest payments.

Overall, the relationship between PE and EPS is not straight-forward. Investors are forward looking, while PE and EPS ratios can only be captured after-the-fact (after investors have already updated their beliefs about a company and after a company releases its earnings). However, our graphs show that the two figures are at least somewhat correlated over a long-time horizon (10+ years), which captures the essence of investing (I.E. investors pay a premium (price) for a company’s current earnings in order to receive a greater return on their investment in the future through increased earnings).

Stock Price vs. Profitability

What moves a stock?

This visualization shows how stock prices respond to changes in a company’s profitability as measured by gross profit (profit after taking into account cost of making and delivering goods/services) and operating profit (profit after taking into account ALL expenses like rent and administrative expenses, but before tax or interest payments). As is expected, stock prices tended to rise as profitability rose. However, what was interesting to us is that the degree to which this happened varied between the healthcare and tech industries.

Technology stocks like Apple (AAPL), Nvidia (NVDA), Broadcom (AVGO), Alphabet (GOOGL), etc. demonstrate consistent increases in stock price as profitability increased. On the other hand, this was not always the case for healthcare stocks, where stock prices for many companies such as Johnson and Johnson (JNJ) and Eli Lilly (LLY) among others managed to rise even without drastically different profitability profiles.

We suspect this trend is because technology companies are much younger, and so investors are looking for profitability to prove that these companies’ business models are viable. In this case, achieving and expanding profitability is the primary way that these companies communicate to the market that they are worthy of long-term investments. It was almost always the case that as profitability rose, the prices of tech stocks rose in tandem.

Summary of Findings

From our data visualizations, we've gained crucial insights into the financial landscapes of leading tech and healthcare companies in the S&P 500. Firstly, our analysis of debt-to-equity ratios showcased distinct financial strategies: healthcare firms tend to maintain stable debt levels due to predictable cash flows, while tech companies exhibit a wider range of ratios reflecting diverse growth stages and business models. Secondly, comparisons of liquidity metrics revealed that tech companies generally have higher working capital, indicating better short-term financial health and operational efficiency, whereas healthcare companies may rely more on debt financing strategies.

Investor sentiment and market dynamics were elucidated through interactive visualizations. For instance, our PE vs. EPS analysis highlighted how market expectations influence stock valuations, with investors reacting to both current earnings and future growth potential. Moreover, our exploration of stock price movements in response to changes in profitability underscored industry-specific dynamics, with tech stocks showing stronger correlations between profitability increases and stock price rises compared to mature healthcare companies.

Looking ahead, this project could expand to include a broader range of industries for comparative analysis, incorporate predictive modeling for stock price forecasting, develop risk assessment frameworks using financial ratios, delve deeper into sector-specific insights, and build interactive decision support tools for investors. These avenues offer promising directions for leveraging data visualization to enhance investment decision-making processes and gain actionable insights into market trends and financial fundamentals.
